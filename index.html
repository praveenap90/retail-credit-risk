What Is Retail Credit Risk?

Credit risk is the potential for financial loss when a borrower fails to repay a loan. In the retail (consumer) segment, this spans mortgages, credit cards, auto loans, student loans, and personal loans.

Default Risk

The probability that a borrower will fail to make required payments. The foundation of all credit risk analysis and pricing.

Concentration Risk

Overexposure to a single borrower type, geography, or product creates systemic vulnerability if market conditions shift.

Behavioral Risk

Post-origination patterns — paydown behavior, balance cycling, utilization — drive ongoing portfolio risk assessment.

The 5 C's of Credit

The foundational framework used by lenders to evaluate creditworthiness. Every underwriting model, scorecard, and credit policy traces back to one or more of these five dimensions.

C1
Character

The borrower's willingness to repay — assessed through credit history, payment track record, length of credit relationships, and bureau data. The "will they pay?" dimension.

Key Signals
  • FICO / VantageScore
  • Derogatory marks, bankruptcies
  • Payment history (35% of FICO)
  • Delinquency history with prior lenders
C2
Capacity

The borrower's ability to repay based on current income and existing obligations. The "can they pay?" dimension. The most operationally complex C to verify.

Key Signals
  • Debt-to-Income (DTI) ratio
  • Payment-to-Income (PTI)
  • Verified income (W-2, tax returns, bank statements)
  • Employment stability & tenure
C3
Capital

The borrower's net worth and financial reserves. Acts as a buffer against income disruption. Particularly important in mortgage and business lending decisions.

Key Signals
  • Cash reserves / months of payment coverage
  • Retirement & investment accounts
  • Down payment amount (skin in the game)
  • Net worth relative to loan size
C4
Collateral

Assets pledged as security for the loan. Reduces LGD by providing recovery value in the event of default. Absent in unsecured products (credit cards, personal loans).

Key Signals
  • Loan-to-Value (LTV) ratio
  • Combined LTV (CLTV) for HELOCs
  • Collateral type & liquidation ease
  • Appraised value vs. purchase price
C5
Conditions

The macroeconomic and industry environment in which the loan is made. Even creditworthy borrowers default in severe downturns. Captured in stress testing and macro-overlay models.

Key Signals
  • Unemployment rate & GDP growth
  • Interest rate environment
  • Home Price Index (HPI) trends
  • Loan purpose & use of proceeds

Core Metrics & KPIs

The quantitative backbone of every credit risk function. These metrics drive reporting, stress testing, provisioning, and portfolio management decisions.

PD
Probability of Default

The likelihood a borrower will default within a defined horizon (typically 12 months). Core input to all credit models and CECL estimates.

PD = Defaults / Total Accounts
LGD
Loss Given Default

The proportion of exposure that is lost after recovery efforts, net of collateral proceeds and collections recoveries.

LGD = 1 − Recovery Rate
EAD
Exposure at Default

Total value at risk when default occurs. For revolving products, includes drawn and undrawn commitments via a Credit Conversion Factor (CCF).

EAD = Balance + CCF × Unused Limit
EL
Expected Loss

The average anticipated credit loss. Used in pricing, provisioning, and CECL allowance calculations under ASC 326.

EL = PD × LGD × EAD
NCO
Net Charge-Off Rate

Annualized losses net of recoveries as a percent of average balances. The headline loss metric reported to investors and regulators.

NCO% = (Gross C/O − Recoveries) / Avg Bal
DPD
Days Past Due

Delinquency buckets: 30, 60, 90+ DPD. FDIC defines charge-off at 180 DPD for credit cards; 120 DPD for most closed-end products.

Delinq. Rate = DPD30+ Accounts / Total

Other Key Underwriting Ratios

Debt-to-Income (DTI)

Monthly debt obligations ÷ gross monthly income. QM mortgage limit: 43%. Industry best practice: <36%.

Loan-to-Value (LTV)

Loan balance ÷ collateral value. Conventional mortgage threshold: 80% (PMI required above). Risk rises sharply at LTV >90%.

Payment-to-Income (PTI)

Monthly payment ÷ gross monthly income. Used in auto lending. Typical threshold: <15–20% of gross income.

Credit Utilization

Revolving balance ÷ credit limit. The #2 FICO factor. >30% utilization signals stress; >70% is high risk.

Credit Scoring & Models

From traditional scorecards to machine learning, lenders use a spectrum of approaches to quantify borrower risk.

Weight of Evidence (WoE) Scorecard

WoE scorecards have been the industry standard since the 1970s. They bin continuous variables, assign evidence weights based on event/non-event distributions, and sum points into a final score. Fully auditable and regulator-approved.

WoEi = ln( % Eventsi / % Non-Eventsi ) Information Value (IV) = Σ (% Eventsi − % Non-Eventsi) × WoEi IV Interpretation: < 0.02 Useless predictor 0.02–0.1 Weak predictor 0.1–0.3 Medium predictor 0.3–0.5 Strong predictor > 0.5 Suspicious (check for leakage)
Advantages
  • Fully interpretable and auditable
  • Easy regulatory model validation
  • Handles missing data naturally
  • Monotonic transformation enforced
Limitations
  • Manual binning and expert judgment
  • Linear relationships only
  • Misses complex variable interactions
  • Requires champion/challenger framework

Statistical Modeling Approaches

Logistic Regression

The industry workhorse. Estimates P(Default) via a sigmoid function. Interpretable, stable, and widely accepted by regulators. Output directly usable as probability estimate.

log(p/1−p) = β⊂0; + β⊂1;X⊂1; + … + βnXn

Survival Analysis

Models time-to-default rather than binary outcome. Cox proportional hazards accounts for censored data and maturation effects. Essential for vintage-based loss forecasting.

h(t|X) = h⊂0;(t) × exp(βX)

Tobit / Truncated Models

Used when the dependent variable is bounded (e.g., LGD constrained to 0–100%). Corrects for selection bias in recovery data, which is only observed for defaulted accounts.

Probit Model

Alternative to logistic regression using the normal CDF instead of logistic function. Common in academic literature; logit typically preferred in industry for coefficient interpretability.

ML models often outperform traditional scorecards in discrimination power (Gini/AUC) but require additional governance. Explainability tools like SHAP are now standard for fair lending compliance.

Scorecard Development Process

Building a production-grade credit scorecard is a structured, multi-phase process governed by statistical rigor, regulatory requirements, and business constraints.

01

Data Acquisition & Sampling

Extract a development sample spanning 18–36 months of originations with sufficient observation window (typically 12–18 months) to observe performance. Define bad (default/charge-off) and good (non-default) flags. Handle class imbalance — default rates are typically 2–10%.

Sample size: minimum 1,500 bads In-time vs. out-of-time validation split Population stability check
02

Exploratory Data Analysis & Variable Selection

Profile all candidate variables for missing rates, outliers, and univariate discrimination. Calculate Information Value (IV) for each candidate. Eliminate data-leakage variables (any data captured after performance observation period begins).

Target IV > 0.1 for inclusion Check pairwise VIF for multicollinearity Business logic review on all variables
03

Variable Transformation (WoE Binning)

Bin continuous variables into monotonic segments using fine/coarse classing. Calculate WoE for each bin. Ensure business logic: higher utilization → higher WoE → higher risk. Group sparse categories in categorical variables.

Minimum 5% of observations per bin Separate missing values as own bin Enforce monotonic trend where logical
04

Model Fitting & Variable Selection

Regress bad flag on WoE-transformed variables using logistic regression. Apply stepwise selection (forward, backward, or both). Target 8–15 variables. Confirm each coefficient is positive (higher WoE = higher log-odds of bad).

Remove variables with p-value > 0.05 Check VIF < 10 for final variables Confirm positive coefficient direction
05

Score Scaling & Calibration

Convert log-odds output to a scaled score (typically 300–850 or 0–1000). Common scaling: 20 points per doubling of odds, score of 600 at odds of 50:1 good:bad. Calibrate PD output using Platt scaling or isotonic regression if necessary.

Score = Offset + Factor × ln(p_good / p_bad) Factor = PDO / ln(2) [PDO = Points to Double Odds] Offset = BaseScore − Factor × ln(BaseOdds)
06

Validation & Performance Testing

Evaluate on held-out OOT (out-of-time) sample. Report full set of discrimination and calibration metrics. Conduct sensitivity analysis and business-rule override impact analysis.

Target Gini > 40% on OOT sample KS statistic: where max separation occurs Hosmer-Lemeshow for calibration
07

Independent Model Validation (SR 11-7)

An independent team (not the developers) performs conceptual soundness review, replication testing, benchmarking against challenger models, and documentation review. Required for all material models at regulated institutions.

Findings logged in model inventory Restrictions if material issues found Annual ongoing monitoring required
08

Production Deployment & Monitoring

Implement score in decisioning system. Begin champion/challenger testing. Track PSI monthly (score distribution shift), CSI by variable (characteristic drift), and Gini on rolling development windows. Trigger recalibration if PSI > 0.2.

PSI monthly; CSI quarterly Recalibrate if PSI > 0.2 Redevelop if Gini drops > 5 points

Model Performance Metrics Reference

Discrimination Metrics
  • Gini Coefficient — 2 × AUC − 1. Target >40%. Measures rank-ordering ability.
  • KS Statistic — Max separation between cumulative good/bad curves. Target >35%.
  • AUC-ROC — Area under ROC curve. Target >0.7. Equivalent to Gini by 2×AUC−1.
  • Lift Chart — Actual bad rate vs. random expectation at each score decile.
Calibration & Stability Metrics
  • Hosmer-Lemeshow Test — Chi-square test of predicted vs. actual default rates across deciles.
  • PSI — Population Stability Index. <0.1 = stable; 0.1–0.2 = monitor; >0.2 = investigate.
  • CSI — Characteristic Stability Index per input variable. Identifies drift source.
  • Accuracy Ratio — Alternative name for Gini; used in European Basel frameworks.

Collections Strategy Framework

Champion/Challenger

Split traffic between current best strategy (champion) and new approach (challenger). Typically 80/20 split. Enables continuous optimization without sacrificing portfolio returns.

Right-Party Contact (RPC)

The percentage of outbound calls reaching the actual borrower. Typically 20–40% for phone contact. Key driver of promise-to-pay rates and overall cure efficiency.

Roll Rate Analysis

Probability that accounts migrate between delinquency buckets. E.g., 60% of 30 DPD accounts "roll" to 60 DPD. Used to forecast future losses from current delinquency pipeline.

Portfolio Management

Active portfolio management balances risk appetite, return objectives, regulatory capital, and concentration limits across the credit portfolio lifecycle.

Credit Limit Management

Proactively adjust credit lines based on behavioral scores, bureau refreshes, and macroeconomic signals. Line increases reward good behavior; proactive decreases ("CLDs") reduce exposure ahead of anticipated defaults.

Portfolio Segmentation

Divide the portfolio into homogeneous risk segments (by score band, product, vintage, geography) to enable targeted strategies, differentiated loss forecasting, and efficient capital allocation.

Concentration Monitoring

Track exposure concentration by state, employer, industry, FICO band, and origination channel. Enforce pre-approved concentration limits to prevent systemic loss from correlated risk factors.

Portfolio Analytics Framework

Vintage Analysis

Track cumulative loss curves by origination cohort. Identify deteriorating vintages early. Benchmark new production against historical norms to detect underwriting drift.

Roll Rate / Migration

Monitor delinquency bucket migration rates (e.g., % of 30 DPD that "roll" to 60 DPD). Changes in roll rates are the earliest leading indicator of loss forecast changes.

Loss Forecasting

Bottom-up: segment-level PD × LGD × EAD. Top-down: macro-driven regression models. Reconcile against roll-rate pipeline. 12-month and lifetime horizon required under CECL.

Early Warning Indicators

Monthly triggers: rising 30+ DPD rates, declining behavioral scores, increased bureau inquiries, utilization spikes, payment pattern changes (minimum payers trending up).

Capital & CECL Provisioning

Translate loss forecasts into CECL allowance estimates. Stress test ALLL under macro scenarios. Ensure capital buffers are sufficient under Severely Adverse scenario.

Profitability Analysis

Measure RAROC by segment: (Revenue − Funding Cost − OpEx − EL) / Economic Capital. Segments below hurdle rate trigger repricing, cutoff tightening, or wind-down decisions.

Collections & Recovery

Collections is the process of recovering payments from delinquent borrowers. Recovery refers to funds received on previously charged-off balances — both directly impact the Net Charge-Off rate.

Collections Waterfall

Borrower contact attempts escalate through a structured sequence based on DPD bucket, balance tier, and risk score. Higher-value accounts with good behavioral history receive more intensive and earlier intervention.

Automated Reminder (1–15 DPD): SMS, email, app notification
Soft Collections (16–30 DPD): Outbound calls, payment plans offered
Intensive Collections (31–60 DPD): Increased frequency, hardship programs
Agency Referral (61–90 DPD): 3rd-party collectors, settlement offers
Legal / Charge-Off (90–180 DPD): Legal review, write-off, debt sale

Recovery Channels

After charge-off, accounts can recover value through multiple channels. Recovery rates vary widely by product, balance size, and time since charge-off.

Internal Recovery
Recovery: 15–25%

Bank's own collections team continues pursuit post charge-off. Best economics but constrained by capacity.

3rd-Party Agency
Agency commission: 25–40% of recovery

Contingency-fee collectors. FDCPA compliance required. Right-party contact rates decline with account age.

Debt Sale
Sale price: 5–15¢ per dollar

One-time clean sale to debt buyer. Immediate cash, no future resource, transfers all compliance obligations.

Legal Judgment
Used for balances > $2,000–$5,000

Lawsuit → wage garnishment or bank levy. Cost-effective only for larger balances. Multi-year timeline.

Collections Performance KPIs

RPC
Right-Party Contact Rate

% of outbound attempts that reach the actual borrower. 20–40% typical. Directly drives promise-to-pay and cure rates.

RPC% = Contacts / Attempts
PTP
Promise-to-Pay Rate

% of right-party contacts who commit to a payment. Kept PTP rate measures actual follow-through. Benchmark: 60–70% kept.

PTP% = Promises / RPC Contacts
CUR
Cure Rate

% of delinquent accounts that return to current status within 1–2 payment cycles. Key metric for early-stage collections efficiency.

Cure% = Cured / Total DPD30+

Hardship & Modification Programs

Short-Term Hardship Programs
  • Payment deferral (1–3 months): payments skipped and added to end of term
  • Minimum payment reduction: temporarily lower required payment
  • Late fee waiver: goodwill gesture to re-engage at-risk borrowers
  • Interest rate reduction: temporary APR cap for enrolled customers
Long-Term Modification Programs
  • Permanent interest rate reduction (e.g., CCCS-referred programs)
  • Principal forgiveness / settlement (typically 40–70% of balance)
  • Extended repayment terms: reduce monthly payment obligation
  • Debt Management Plan (DMP): third-party nonprofit administration

Stress Testing Framework

Stress testing quantifies potential credit losses under adverse economic scenarios. Required by regulation for large banks; essential for risk appetite calibration at all lenders.

Baseline
Consensus economic forecast. GDP growth ~2%, unemployment ~4.5%. Used for budget planning and base CECL provisioning.
GDP: +2.0%Unemployment: 4.5%HPI: +3%

Satellite Model Architecture

Macro → Credit Risk Linkages
  • Unemployment rate: Strongest driver of consumer PD (positive relationship)
  • GDP growth: Inverse relationship with default rates; lag effect ~2–4 quarters
  • Home Price Index: Key driver of mortgage LGD and HELOC default
  • Yield curve / credit spreads: Drive refinancing behavior and strategic default
  • Consumer confidence index: Leading indicator for revolving utilization
Model Types by Loss Component
  • PD Model: OLS or ECM regression of historical default rates on macro variables. Separate models by product/segment.
  • LGD Model: Regression of recovery rates on collateral values, macro conditions. Beta regression common (0–1 bounded).
  • EAD Model: CCF (Credit Conversion Factor) regression for revolving lines. Draws often spike pre-default.
  • PPNR Model: Revenue and expense models linked to rate environment, volume projections.
Key Stress Testing Pitfall: Satellite models calibrated on recent benign history may understate stress losses. Backtesting against 2008–2009 GFC data is essential to validate model behavior in severe stress, where macro–credit relationships may become non-linear.

US Regulatory Framework

Retail credit is among the most heavily regulated financial activities. Compliance failures carry legal, reputational, and financial consequences.

ECOA
Equal Credit Opportunity Act (1974)

  • Incurred Loss: Reserve only when loss is "probable and estimable" — typically triggered by delinquency

Foundational
Strong Answer

Because collateral reduces lender risk and potential loss severity. The lender has a recovery mechanism if the borrower defaults, so the expected loss is lower — and lower expected loss translates directly into lower required return (APR).

Intermediate
Strong Answer

The three quantitative components are Probability of Default (PD), Exposure at Default (EAD), and Loss Given Default (LGD). Together they produce Expected Loss: EL = PD × LGD × EAD. In secured lending, collateral value significantly impacts LGD. In unsecured, PD and EAD are the dominant drivers.

Expected Loss = PD × LGD × EAD

Foundational
Strong Answer

What is credit risk?
Foundational
Strong Answer

Credit risk is the possibility that a borrower will fail to meet repayment obligations, resulting in financial loss to the lender. It is quantified through three components — PD (probability of default), LGD (loss given default), and EAD (exposure at default) — which together produce Expected Loss: EL = PD × LGD × EAD.

DefaultFinancial LossPD × LGD × EADLender Exposure
What is Probability of Default (PD)?
Foundational
Strong Answer

PD measures the likelihood that a borrower will default over a specific time horizon — typically 12 months for application scoring, or lifetime for CECL provisioning. It is estimated using credit scores, behavioral data, and statistical models (logistic regression, scorecards). Higher PD = higher risk = higher required return or tighter underwriting.

PD = Historical Defaults / Total Accounts in segment
Target: segmented by score band, product, vintage
What is Loss Given Default (LGD)?
Foundational
Strong Answer

LGD represents the percentage of exposure a lender expects to lose after accounting for recoveries once a borrower defaults. LGD = 1 − Recovery Rate. In secured lending, collateral reduces LGD significantly. Unsecured products typically have LGD of 75–100% since there is no asset to liquidate.

LGD = 1 − Recovery Rate
Secured auto: ~40–60%  |  Unsecured card: ~75–90%
What is Exposure at Default (EAD)?
Foundational
Strong Answer

EAD is the total amount the lender is exposed to when a borrower defaults. For installment loans (auto, mortgage), EAD is the outstanding balance. For revolving credit (cards, HELOCs), EAD includes drawn balance plus a Credit Conversion Factor applied to the undrawn limit — because borrowers often draw down heavily before defaulting.

EAD (revolving) = Balance + CCF × Unused Limit
CCF typically 50–80% for credit cards
How is Expected Loss calculated?
Foundational
Strong Answer

Expected Loss is calculated as PD × LGD × EAD. It represents the average anticipated credit loss from a portfolio over a defined horizon. EL is used in CECL provisioning (to set the allowance), risk-based pricing (to determine the minimum APR needed to cover losses), and economic capital allocation across products.

EL = PD × LGD × EAD
e.g. PD 5% × LGD 60% × EAD $20,000 = $600 expected loss
What is unexpected loss?
Intermediate
Strong Answer

Unexpected loss (UL) represents the volatility around expected losses — the risk that actual losses will exceed the average EL. Banks hold regulatory capital (CET1 buffer) specifically to absorb unexpected losses and remain solvent under stress. UL drives economic capital requirements: a higher-volatility portfolio requires more capital held in reserve.

Risk appetite defines the amount and type of risk an organization is willing to accept to achieve its business objectives. It is formally documented in a Risk Appetite Statement and translated into quantitative limits: maximum NCO rate, DPD thresholds, concentration limits, and RAROC hurdle rates. Risk appetite governs underwriting cutoffs, portfolio mix decisions, and capital planning.

Intermediate

Concentration risk occurs when a portfolio is overly exposed to a specific segment, geography, borrower type, or product. If conditions deteriorate in that area, losses can be disproportionately large. Lenders manage concentration risk through exposure limits (e.g., max 20% in any single state), diversification across products, and stress testing correlated scenarios.

Foundational
Strong Answer

Diversification reduces credit risk by spreading exposure across different borrower segments, products, geographies, and industries so that losses in one area do not overwhelm the portfolio. If a lender loosens criteria or raises credit limits, higher-risk borrowers disproportionately take up the offer, increasing portfolio average PD. It is a key risk in pre-approved offers, direct mail campaigns, and subprime product launches.

A PD model predicts the likelihood that a borrower will default within a specified time horizon — typically 12 months for account management, or lifetime for CECL provisioning. PD models use logistic regression or gradient boosting on bureau data, payment history, income, utilization, and behavioral signals. Output is a calibrated probability (0–1) that is monitored for stability using PSI and backtested against actual default rates.

PD = P(Default within horizon | borrower characteristics)
Validated using: AUC, Gini, KS, Hosmer-Lemeshow
What is an LGD model?
Intermediate
Strong Answer

An LGD model estimates the percentage of exposure a lender expects to lose after recoveries once a borrower defaults. LGD = 1 − Recovery Rate. For secured lending, LGD depends on collateral value (LTV, depreciation, auction proceeds) and repossession costs. For unsecured lending, LGD is driven by collections effectiveness, time since default, and balance size. Beta regression is common since LGD is bounded between 0 and 1.

LGD = 1 − Recovery Rate
Secured auto: 30–60%  |  Unsecured card: 75–100%
What is an Exposure at Default (EAD) model?
Intermediate
Strong Answer

An EAD model estimates the total amount outstanding at the time of borrower default. For installment loans, EAD ≈ current balance (minimal uncertainty). For revolving credit (cards, HELOCs), EAD requires a Credit Conversion Factor (CCF) to estimate how much of the undrawn limit will be drawn before default — borrowers typically draw down credit lines pre-default, making EAD > current balance.

EAD (revolving) = Balance + CCF × Unused Limit
CCF for cards: typically 50–80% of undrawn line

Unlike application scores (point-in-time at origination), behavioral scores are refreshed monthly on existing accounts. Logistic regression is interpretable (coefficients directly show variable direction and magnitude), stable (less prone to overfitting than complex models), produces calibrated probability outputs (directly usable as PD), and is widely accepted by regulators. What is a decision tree?
Foundational
Strong Answer

A decision tree recursively splits data into subgroups based on predictor variables that best separate good from bad outcomes. Each split maximizes information gain (Gini impurity or entropy). Decision trees are highly interpretable — the splits translate directly into business rules — but are unstable (small data changes cause large structural changes) and prone to overfitting. Primarily used for segment discovery and policy rule development in credit risk, rather than as standalone production models.

What is random forest?
Intermediate
Strong Answer

Random forest combines multiple decision trees — each trained on a random bootstrap sample with a random subset of features — and averages their predictions. This ensemble approach reduces overfitting and variance while maintaining the ability to capture non-linear patterns. Feature importance scores are derived from average impurity reduction across trees. Less interpretable than logistic regression but more so than gradient boosting; useful as a challenger model or for feature selection.

What is gradient boosting?
Intermediate
Strong Answer

Gradient boosting sequentially adds weak learners (shallow decision trees), each one correcting the residual errors of the previous model. XGBoost and LightGBM are dominant implementations. Produces the highest discrimination (Gini/AUC) on tabular credit data. Requires careful hyperparameter tuning (learning rate, tree depth, regularization) to prevent overfitting. In credit risk, SHAP values are required for adverse action reason code generation and regulatory model documentation.

What is overfitting?
Foundational
Strong Answer

Overfitting occurs when a model learns the noise and idiosyncrasies of the training data rather than true underlying patterns — producing excellent in-sample performance but poor out-of-sample generalization. In credit risk, overfitting is detected by comparing in-sample Gini to out-of-time (OOT) Gini: a large drop (e.g. 55% in-sample vs 40% OOT) signals overfitting. Mitigations include regularization (L1/L2), cross-validation, reducing model complexity, and larger training samples.

What is underfitting?
Foundational
Strong Answer

Underfitting occurs when a model is too simple to capture meaningful relationships in the data — producing poor performance both in-sample and out-of-sample. A logistic regression with only 2 variables on a complex portfolio will underfit. Symptoms include low Gini (<30%), flat lift curves, and residuals that show systematic patterns. Solutions include adding relevant features, using more flexible algorithms, or better WoE binning to capture non-linear variable relationships.

Feature engineering creates predictive variables from raw data to improve model performance. In credit risk: computing utilization ratios from balance and limit data, creating payment ratio (payment made / minimum due), time since last delinquency, balance trend over 3/6/12 months, number of trades opened in last 6 months, income stability proxy from pay frequency. What is class imbalance?
Intermediate
Strong Answer

Class imbalance occurs when default cases are much fewer than non-default cases — typically 2–10% of a portfolio. This causes models to be biased toward predicting non-default, producing high accuracy but poor sensitivity to actual defaults. Solutions include oversampling minority class (SMOTE), undersampling majority class, adjusting class weights, or using probability calibration. The development sample should have at least 1,500 bad accounts for stable scorecard estimation.

In credit risk, interpretability is a regulatory requirement: ECOA/FCRA mandate that adverse action notices provide principal reasons for denial, which requires knowing each variable's contribution. SHAP values fulfill this for ML models. Logistic regression with WoE is inherently interpretable through coefficients and scorecard points.

What is AUC/ROC?
Intermediate
Strong Answer

AUC-ROC (Area Under the Receiver Operating Characteristic Curve) measures a model's ability to distinguish between good and bad borrowers across all classification thresholds. AUC = 0.5 is random (no discrimination); AUC = 1.0 is perfect. In credit risk, AUC >0.70 is generally acceptable; >0.80 is strong. Related to Gini coefficient by: Gini = 2 × AUC − 1. AUC is threshold-independent — it evaluates rank-ordering ability across the entire score range.

AUC >0.70 = Acceptable  |  >0.80 = Strong
Gini = 2 × AUC − 1
What is KS statistic?
Intermediate
Strong Answer

The KS (Kolmogorov-Smirnov) statistic measures the maximum separation between cumulative distributions of good and bad borrowers at any given score threshold. It identifies the score cut-point where the model achieves its highest discriminatory power. KS >35% is generally considered acceptable for production credit models. KS is particularly useful for setting score cutoffs in approval policies and for explaining model performance to business stakeholders.

KS = max |F_good(x) − F_bad(x)| across all score thresholds
Target: KS >35%
What is Gini coefficient?
Intermediate
Strong Answer

The Gini coefficient measures model discriminatory power derived from the ROC/CAP curve: Gini = 2 × AUC − 1. Range 0–1 (or 0–100%). A Gini of 0 means the model has no discriminatory power; 1.0 is perfect. Target Gini >40% for production scorecards; >50% is strong. The Gini should be monitored on a rolling OOT window — a drop >5 points from development Gini triggers a model review under most governance frameworks.

Gini = 2 × AUC − 1
Target >40% OOT  |  Review if drop >5 pts
What is a confusion matrix?
Intermediate
Strong Answer

A confusion matrix evaluates classification performance at a specific score threshold using: True Positives (correctly predicted bads), False Positives (good borrowers predicted as bad — Type I error), False Negatives (bad borrowers predicted as good — Type II error), and True Negatives (correctly predicted goods). In credit risk, Type II errors (approving bad borrowers) are typically more costly than Type I errors (rejecting good borrowers), so the threshold is calibrated to reflect that asymmetry.

What is model calibration?
Intermediate
Strong Answer

Calibration ensures that a model's predicted probabilities align closely with actual observed outcomes. A well-calibrated PD model where the model predicts 5% PD should show ~5% actual defaults in that score band. Hosmer-Lemeshow test formally assesses calibration by comparing predicted vs. actual default rates across deciles. Poor calibration means the model's PD outputs cannot be directly used for CECL provisioning without recalibration (Platt scaling or isotonic regression).

What is back-testing?
Intermediate
Strong Answer

Back-testing compares predicted model outcomes against actual historical performance — asking "how well would this model have performed if deployed in the past?" In credit risk, back-testing is applied to PD models (predicted vs actual default rates by score band), stress test models (projected vs actual NCO in prior downturns), and CECL models (allowance vs actual loss emergence). Back-testing against the 2008–2009 GFC data is the standard stress model validation benchmark.

What is Population Stability Index (PSI)?
Intermediate
Strong Answer

PSI measures whether the population distribution of a model's scores or inputs has shifted significantly between the development period and the current deployment period. PSI <0.1 = stable (no action); 0.1–0.2 = investigate (monitor closely); >0.2 = significant shift (model may need recalibration or redevelopment). PSI is calculated monthly as: PSI = Σ (Actual% − Expected%) × ln(Actual%/Expected%) across score bands. It is the primary model monitoring metric.

Intermediate
Strong Answer

Model drift occurs when model performance deteriorates due to changes in customer behavior, economic conditions, or data generating processes — meaning the relationships the model learned during development no longer hold. Detected through falling Gini on rolling OOT windows, rising PSI/CSI, or increasing divergence between predicted and actual default rates. Drift requires model recalibration (adjust intercept or score offset) or full redevelopment if structural relationships have changed.

What are Type I and Type II errors?
Foundational
Strong Answer

Type I error (false positive) means falsely rejecting a good borrower — the model predicts default but the borrower would have repaid. Type II error (false negative) means approving a bad borrower — the model predicts repayment but the borrower defaults. In credit risk, Type II errors are typically more costly (actual credit loss) than Type I errors (lost revenue opportunity). The classification threshold is set to reflect this asymmetry, and fair lending laws require monitoring Type I error rates across demographic groups.

What is a credit scorecard?
Foundational
Strong Answer

A credit scorecard is a statistically derived model that assigns a numerical risk score to a borrower based on their characteristics. It is typically built using WoE-transformed logistic regression and scaled to a points-based output (e.g., 300–850 or 0–1000). Each characteristic (payment history, utilization, DPD history) contributes a positive or negative points value. The total score drives approve/decline decisions, credit limit assignment, and risk-based pricing. Fully auditable and regulator-approved.

Strong Answer

Common scorecard variables include: payment history (ever 30/60/90 DPD, months since last delinquency), utilization ratio (revolving balance / limit), derogatory marks (bankruptcies, charge-offs, collections), credit inquiries (hard pulls in last 6–12 months), DTI and income ratios, account age (age of oldest/newest account), credit mix (revolving vs installment vs mortgage), and balance trend. What is Weight of Evidence (WoE)?
Intermediate
Strong Answer

WoE transforms categorical or binned continuous variables into a predictive numerical value based on the log ratio of good and bad event rates in each bin. WoE = ln(% Bads in bin / % Goods in bin). Positive WoE = lower-risk bin; negative WoE = higher-risk bin. WoE encoding handles missing values naturally (separate bin), enforces monotonic relationships, and enables logistic regression to work effectively on mixed variable types. The quality of WoE binning is the most important step in scorecard development.

WoE = ln(Distribution of Bads / Distribution of Goods)
Positive WoE = lower risk; Negative WoE = higher risk
What is Information Value (IV)?
Intermediate
Strong Answer

IV quantifies a variable's overall predictive power in distinguishing good from bad borrowers. IV = Σ (% Bads − % Goods) × WoE across all bins. Scale: IV <0.02 = useless; 0.02–0.1 = weak; 0.1–0.3 = medium; 0.3–0.5 = strong; >0.5 = suspicious (potential data leakage). Variables are ranked by IV during feature selection; target IV >0.1 for inclusion. High IV variables (payment history, DPD) should always be included in scorecards.

IV = Σ (% Bads − % Goods)ᵢ × WoEᵢ
>0.3 Strong | >0.5 Suspicious (check leakage)

Bureau scores also enable comparison across applicants on a common scale, power adverse action reason codes, and serve as the primary input for risk-based pricing tiers.

Foundational

Utilization ratio is the percentage of available revolving credit that is currently being used: balance / credit limit. It is the second most important FICO factor (30% of score) and a powerful default predictor. Borrowers above 30% utilization show elevated delinquency rates; above 70% is high risk and often signals liquidity stress. What is vintage modeling?
Intermediate
Strong Answer

Vintage modeling analyzes portfolio performance cohort-by-cohort based on origination period, plotting cumulative loss rates against loan age (months on book). This enables direct comparison of underwriting quality across different time periods — a 2022 vintage that shows higher losses at month 18 than the 2020 vintage did at the same age signals underwriting deterioration. Vintage analysis is foundational to CECL lifetime loss estimation and to detecting the lagged impact of policy changes.

Vintage NCO(t) = Cumulative losses / Original balance at month t
Compare across origination cohorts at same loan age
What are roll rate models?
Intermediate
Strong Answer

Roll rate models estimate the probability of accounts migrating from one delinquency bucket to the next (or curing back to current) in a given period. A roll rate matrix shows: % of 30 DPD that roll to 60 DPD, cure to current, or stay at 30 DPD. Rising roll rates are the most reliable short-term predictor of future charge-off increases. They feed directly into CECL roll rate methodology and collections strategy design.

Roll Rate 30→60 = Accounts moving 30→60 DPD / Total 30 DPD accounts
Rising roll rate → revise NCO forecast upward
What is transition matrix analysis?
Intermediate
Strong Answer

A transition matrix is a square matrix where entry [i,j] represents the probability of an account moving from state i (e.g., Current) to state j (e.g., 30 DPD) over a defined period. It generalizes roll rates to capture all possible state movements simultaneously. Transition matrices are used in Markov chain loss forecasting, CECL roll rate methodology, and IFRS 9 staging models. The matrix must satisfy row-sum constraints (each row sums to 1) and is estimated from historical migration data.

Why is fairness important in credit models?
Intermediate
Strong Answer

Credit models must avoid discriminatory outcomes to comply with ECOA and the Fair Housing Act. Disparate impact — where a facially neutral model disproportionately harms a protected class (race, sex, national origin, age) — is legally actionable even without discriminatory intent. Models must be tested for disparate impact using regression-based analysis and the four-fifths rule. Variables that serve as proxies for protected characteristics (zip code, surname, name) must be excluded or carefully justified.

What is fair lending risk?
Intermediate
Strong Answer

Fair lending risk arises when lending decisions, pricing, or policies disproportionately impact protected groups under ECOA or the Fair Housing Act. Two legal theories: Disparate Treatment (intentional discrimination — treating a protected group differently) and Disparate Impact (unintentional discrimination through neutral policies with disproportionate adverse effects). The CFPB and DOJ actively supervise and enforce fair lending; enforcement actions have resulted in settlements exceeding $100M (e.g., Ally Financial auto pricing, 2013).

What is model governance?
Intermediate
Strong Answer

Model governance provides the framework for oversight of model development, validation, implementation, and ongoing monitoring. It defines who can build models, what standards apply, who validates them independently, what committee approves deployment, how models are monitored post-deployment, and when they must be redeveloped. Under SR 11-7, every material credit model must be in the model inventory, independently validated, and subject to periodic performance review. Governance failures are a common finding in regulatory examinations.

What is adverse action reasoning?
Intermediate
Strong Answer

Adverse action notices explain why a borrower was denied credit or received less favorable terms — required by ECOA and FCRA within 30 days. The notice must state the principal reasons (typically 4) based on model outputs. For logistic regression/WoE scorecards, reason codes map directly to the highest-scoring (most adverse) variable contributions. For ML models (XGBoost), SHAP values are used to derive reason codes. Generating legally compliant adverse action codes from complex ML models is a major challenge in model deployment.

Why is model documentation important?
Foundational
Strong Answer

Documentation supports transparency, auditability, regulatory compliance, and business continuity. SR 11-7 requires documentation covering: model purpose and intended use, data sources and data quality assessment, methodology and assumptions, performance metrics (in-sample and OOT), limitations and known weaknesses, and validation findings. Without documentation, models cannot be validated or approved for use — they become "black boxes" that regulators, auditors, and successors cannot assess. Poor documentation is the most common model risk management deficiency.

What is challenger model validation?
Intermediate
Strong Answer

Challenger model validation independently tests whether an alternative modeling approach outperforms the current production (champion) model. A challenger model — typically a more complex algorithm or a model retrained on recent data — is built by developers and then evaluated by an independent validation team against the champion on OOT samples. Benchmarking is a SR 11-7 requirement: validation must assess whether better alternatives exist. If the challenger consistently outperforms the champion, it becomes the basis for model redevelopment.

Why is monitoring important after deployment?
Foundational
Strong Answer

Models can degrade over time due to changing customer behavior, economic conditions, or data generating processes — a phenomenon called model drift. Post-deployment monitoring tracks PSI (population distribution shift), CSI (characteristic-level drift), Gini on rolling OOT windows, and calibration (predicted vs actual default rates). Monitoring triggers: PSI >0.2 → investigation; Gini drop >5 pts → model review; calibration ratio outside 0.8–1.2 → recalibration. Monthly monitoring is standard; quarterly reporting to MRM committee.

What is conceptual soundness?
Intermediate
Strong Answer

Conceptual soundness evaluates whether a model's design, assumptions, and methodology are theoretically justified and aligned with business objectives and credit risk theory. Under SR 11-7, it is the first pillar of model validation. A conceptually sound credit model uses appropriate outcome definitions (default defined correctly), suitable modeling technique for the problem (logistic regression for binary outcome), economically meaningful variables (no spurious correlations or data leakage), and assumptions that are reasonable and documented. A statistically powerful model that is conceptually unsound should not be approved for production use.
