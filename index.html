Chapter 01

Foundations & Terminology

Retail credit risk is the potential loss a lender faces when a borrower — an individual rather than a corporation — fails to repay. Understanding the building blocks is essential before tackling advanced models.

The Credit Risk Trinity

Every credit risk calculation ultimately reduces to three core components. These parameters drive provisioning, pricing, capital and strategy.

PD — Probability of Default
The likelihood that a borrower will fail to meet contractual obligations over a given horizon (typically 12 months). Expressed as a percentage.
PD = P(default | borrower characteristics)
LGD — Loss Given Default
The fraction of the exposure the lender loses if default occurs, net of recoveries and collateral. 1 − LGD = Recovery Rate.
LGD = (EAD − Recovery) / EAD
EAD — Exposure at Default
The total outstanding balance expected at the time of default, including drawn balances and estimated future drawdowns on revolving facilities.
EAD = Current Balance + CCF × Undrawn
Expected Loss (EL)
The average credit loss anticipated from a portfolio or facility over a specified period. Used for provisioning and pricing.
EL = PD × LGD × EAD
Unexpected Loss (UL)
Losses beyond the expected level, arising from correlation effects and tail events. UL drives economic capital requirements.
UL ≈ EAD × LGD × √(PD × (1-PD))
Credit Conversion Factor (CCF)
Estimates the proportion of undrawn commitment likely to be drawn prior to default. Key for credit cards and revolving lines.
CCF = (EAD − Current) / Undrawn Limit

Default Definition

The regulatory definition of default under Basel (CRR Article 178) is triggered by either:

  • 90 days past due — the obligor is more than 90 DPD on a material credit obligation (up to 180 days for some retail exposures in certain jurisdictions).
  • Unlikely to pay (UTP) — the institution considers the obligor unlikely to pay without realisation of collateral. Triggers include forbearance, write-down, distressed restructure, or bankruptcy.
Important

The materiality threshold for the 90-DPD test is set by local regulators. In the EU it is the higher of €100 or 1% of total exposure. Institutions must apply default consistently across the obligor's full exposure (contagion rule).

Chapter 02

Origination & Underwriting

Underwriting is the structured process of evaluating a credit application against a lender's risk appetite, culminating in an accept, decline, or refer decision.

The Credit Decision Pipeline

1
Application & Identity Verification
Customer submits application data. KYC/AML checks confirm identity and screen against sanctions lists. Real-time fraud scores applied. Soft credit bureau search may be run at pre-qualification stage.
2
Bureau Pull & Data Enrichment
Hard or soft credit bureau enquiry retrieves trade lines, derogatory marks, payment history, and existing indebtedness. Open Banking transaction data may supplement thin-file applicants.
3
Scorecard & Policy Rules
Application score derived. Automatic declines applied for hard cutoffs (e.g. current default, undischarged bankruptcy). Policy rules remove specific risk segments before manual referral.
4
Affordability Assessment
Debt-to-income (DTI) ratio, net disposable income (NDI), and stress-tested repayments checked against minimum standards. Regulatory requirement in most jurisdictions.
5
Decision Engine Output
Waterfall of rules produces Accept / Decline / Refer. Refer cases route to manual underwriting queues with supporting data packs. Accepted cases proceed to offer and pricing.
6
Offer, Pricing & Limit Setting
Risk-based pricing ties interest rate to PD band. Credit limit assignment uses income, bureau utilisation, and affordability. Customer accepts; facility is booked and monitoring begins.

The 5 Cs of Credit

💡

Character

  • Credit history and bureau score
  • Previous delinquencies or defaults
  • Relationship tenure with lender
  • Repayment behaviour on existing debt
💰

Capacity

  • Monthly income (net / gross)
  • Debt-to-income ratio (DTI)
  • Employment stability and type
  • Net disposable income after commitments
🏠

Collateral

  • Property value and loan-to-value (LTV)
  • Vehicle depreciation schedule
  • Enforceability of security interest
  • Liquidation timeline and recovery costs
📊

Capital

  • Savings and liquid assets
  • Investment portfolio and pension value
  • Property equity outside the loan
  • Ability to absorb income shocks
🌍

Conditions

  • Purpose of loan (secured vs. unsecured)
  • Macroeconomic environment
  • Industry / sector of employment
  • Interest rate environment and stress

Key Affordability Metrics

Debt-to-Income Ratio
DTI = Total Monthly Debt Obligations / Gross Monthly Income × 100

Net Disposable Income
NDI = Net Income − Living Costs − All Debt Servicing

Loan-to-Value (Mortgages)
LTV = Loan Amount / Property Value × 100

Stress Test Payment
Stressed Payment = Balance × (Current Rate + Stress Buffer) / 12

Chapter 03

Credit Scoring

Credit scoring translates borrower attributes into a rank-ordering statistic that predicts the probability of default or delinquency.

Score Types

Application Score
Built at origination using application form data and bureau information. Predicts default within 12–24 months of booking. Drives accept/decline and limit setting.
Behavioural Score
Uses ongoing account behaviour — payments, utilisation, transactions — to refresh risk assessment monthly. Drives limit management and collections strategies.
Bureau Score
Third-party generic score (e.g. FICO, Experian) derived purely from credit file data. Useful as input feature or thin-file proxy; not calibrated to lender's own portfolio.
Collections Score
Predicts whether a delinquent account will self-cure, roll further, or recover. Determines contact prioritisation and collections treatment strategy.
Propensity Score
Predicts likelihood of customer taking a product offer. When combined with a risk score, enables risk-adjusted marketing targeting.
Attrition Score
Predicts probability of pre-payment or account closure. Important for net interest margin (NIM) forecasting and retention strategy.

Scorecard Development Process

1
Data Collection & Sample Design
Define observation window (when booked), performance window (outcome measured), and outcome definition. Extract sample ensuring adequate bad rates (~5–15% ideal). Address through-the-door vs. accepted-only selection bias (reject inference).
2
Variable Selection & WOE Binning
Weight of Evidence (WOE) transforms variables into log-odds space. Information Value (IV) measures predictive power per variable. IV > 0.02 = some value; IV > 0.5 = suspicious (possible data leakage).
3
Logistic Regression (or ML model)
Logistic regression is industry standard for regulatory scorecards due to explainability. Output is log-odds, transformed to a scaled score using Points-to-Double-the-Odds (PDO) scaling convention.
4
Validation & Performance Testing
Gini coefficient (= 2×AUC−1) measures rank-ordering power; target Gini > 40% for application scores. KS statistic measures max separation between goods and bads. Calibration tested via Hosmer-Lemeshow test.
5
Monitoring & Annual Review
Population Stability Index (PSI) tracks score distribution drift. PSI > 0.1 = minor shift (monitor); > 0.2 = significant (investigate); > 0.25 = model likely requires redevelopment.
WOE and IV Formulae
WOE_i = ln(Distribution_Goods_i / Distribution_Bads_i)
IV = Σ (Distribution_Goods_i − Distribution_Bads_i) × WOE_i

Score Scaling (Points-to-Double-the-Odds)
Score = Offset − PDO × log₂(odds)
Typical: Base Score=600, PDO=20, Base Odds=50:1

Gini Coefficient
Gini = 2 × AUC − 1 (ranges 0 to 1; higher = better discrimination)

Population Stability Index
PSI = Σ (Actual% − Expected%) × ln(Actual% / Expected%)

Chapter 04

Portfolio Management

Portfolio management involves monitoring the aggregate risk profile of a credit book, identifying concentrations, and aligning performance with the institution's risk appetite framework.

Core Portfolio Metrics

NPL Ratio
Non-Performing Loans as a proportion of total gross loans. Loans >90 DPD or in default. Key headline indicator tracked by regulators and investors.
NPL% = NPL Balance / Gross Loan Book
Coverage Ratio
Loan loss provisions held as a percentage of NPL balance. Indicates adequacy of provisioning buffer against non-performing exposure.
Coverage = Provisions / NPL Balance
Net Charge-off Rate
Net write-offs (write-offs less recoveries) annualised as a % of average outstanding balances. Common in US retail credit reporting.
NCO Rate = Net Charge-offs / Avg Balances × 4
Roll Rate
Percentage of accounts in a delinquency bucket that move to the next worse bucket in the following period. Core input to loss forecasting models.
Roll = Accounts in bucket B(t+1) / Bucket A(t)
Vintage Analysis
Tracks cumulative default rate of accounts booked in the same period as they season. Reveals origination quality and portfolio seasoning effects over time.
RAROC
Risk-Adjusted Return on Capital. Compares risk-adjusted income to economic capital consumed. Enables comparison of profitability across products and segments.
RAROC = (Revenue − EL − OpEx) / Econ. Capital

Vintage Analysis

Reading a Vintage Curve

Vintage curves plot cumulative default rate (y-axis) against months-on-book (x-axis) for each booking cohort. Key patterns to monitor:

  • Curves rising steeply in months 6–18 signal adverse selection or deteriorating underwriting standards
  • A recent cohort tracking above older vintages at the same seasoning point is an early warning signal requiring investigation
  • Flat curves after month 30 indicate maturity; most losses have been realised — useful for LGD and EAD calibration
  • Diverging curves by origination channel (direct vs. broker) reveal distribution quality differences

Concentration Risk

Definition

Concentration risk arises when a portfolio has excessive exposure to a single borrower, geographic region, industry, product type, or risk segment. Diversification reduces unexpected loss; concentration amplifies tail risk and correlation.

Chapter 05

Collections & Recovery

Collections is the discipline of managing accounts once they become delinquent — from the first missed payment through to legal enforcement and debt sale.

Delinquency Stages

!
Early Arrears — 1–30 DPD
Self-cure rate ~60–70%. Automated SMS/email prompts. Focus on "can pay, won't pay" identification. Promise-to-pay (PTP) arrangements. Minimal cost per case. Collections score drives early prioritisation.
!!
Mid Arrears — 31–89 DPD
Self-cure rate drops to ~30–40%. Proactive outbound calling. Vulnerability assessment and full affordability review. Instalment plans, payment holidays, and formal forbearance offers. IFRS 9 Stage 2 trigger likely.
!!!
Default — 90+ DPD or Unlikely to Pay
Regulatory default triggered. IFRS 9 Stage 3 (lifetime ECL). Formal default notice issued. Debt Management Plan (DMP) or IVA referral. Free debt advice signposting required (UK regulatory obligation).
W
Write-off & Recovery
Typically 150–180 DPD for unsecured; 12–18 months for secured post-repossession. Removed from balance sheet. Recovery via in-house collections, third-party debt purchaser sale, or legal enforcement. Recoveries credited back to reduce realised LGD.

Forbearance & Vulnerability

Forbearance
A concession granted to a borrower in financial difficulty that would not be offered under normal commercial circumstances. Includes rate reduction, payment holiday, term extension, partial capitalisation, or partial write-off.
Unlikely to Pay (UTP)
Default trigger even without 90 DPD if the institution judges the borrower unlikely to pay in full without enforcement of collateral. Includes bankruptcy, write-down, and distressed restructure.
Cure Rate
Percentage of accounts returning to performing status after forbearance. IFRS 9 requires a 2-year post-forbearance probation period before restaging to Stage 1 or removing from Stage 2.
Vulnerable Customer
UK FCA: a customer who, due to personal circumstances, is especially susceptible to harm. Factors include health conditions, life events, financial resilience, and financial capability. Requires tailored treatment.
UK Regulatory Context — Consumer Duty 2023

Under FCA Consumer Duty (PS22/9), firms must deliver good outcomes for all customers including those in financial difficulty. Collections practices must demonstrate fair treatment, accessible communications in plain English, and active signposting to free debt advice (StepChange, Citizens Advice, National Debtline).

Chapter 06

Regulation & Accounting

Credit risk is shaped by two major frameworks: Basel capital regulation (how much capital to hold) and IFRS 9 accounting (how much provision to hold against expected losses).

Basel III Capital Framework

Basel III requires banks to hold sufficient capital against credit risk. For retail portfolios two approaches exist:

🏦

Standardised Approach (SA)

  • Risk weights prescribed by regulator
  • Retail residential mortgages: 35% (LTV ≤80%) to 100%
  • Qualifying revolving retail: 75% risk weight
  • Other retail exposures: 75% risk weight
  • Simpler; less risk-sensitive
  • Used by smaller banks or pre-IRB approval
⚙️

Internal Ratings-Based (IRB)

  • Banks use own PD / LGD / EAD estimates
  • Foundation IRB: regulator sets LGD & EAD
  • Advanced IRB: bank estimates all parameters
  • Capital = f(PD, LGD, EAD, correlation ρ)
  • Requires supervisory approval and robust governance
  • Basel IV output floor: 72.5% of SA by 2028
Basel IRB Capital Formula (Retail)
K = LGD × [N(√(1/(1-R)) × G(PD) + √(R/(1-R)) × G(0.999)) − PD]
R (correlation) = 0.03×(1-e^(-35×PD))/(1-e^(-35)) + 0.16×[1-(1-e^(-35×PD))/(1-e^(-35))]

RWA = K × 12.5 × EAD
Minimum CET1 Capital Required = RWA × 4.5%

IFRS 9 — Expected Credit Loss (ECL)

IFRS 9 (effective 2018) replaced IAS 39 incurred loss with a forward-looking ECL model using three staging buckets:

StageDefinitionECL MeasurementInterest Accrual
Stage 1No significant increase in credit risk since origination (performing)12-month ECLEffective interest on gross balance
Stage 2Significant increase in credit risk (SICR) — e.g. score deterioration, 30+ DPD, watchlist placementLifetime ECLEffective interest on gross balance
Stage 3Credit-impaired — meets default definition (90 DPD, UTP, forbearance)Lifetime ECL (credit-impaired)Effective interest on net (post-provision) balance
SICR — Significant Increase in Credit Risk

SICR triggers the move from Stage 1 to Stage 2 and switches provisioning from 12-month to lifetime ECL. Typical indicators: PD increase ≥200% vs. origination PD, absolute PD threshold, 30 DPD backstop, watchlist placement, or forbearance. Defining SICR criteria involves significant management judgement and is a key area of regulatory scrutiny.

12-Month ECL (Stage 1)
ECL₁₂ = PD₁₂ × LGD × EAD × Discount Factor

Lifetime ECL (Stage 2 & 3)
ECL_Lifetime = Σ (PDₜ × LGD × EADₜ × DFₜ) for t = 1 to remaining life
where PDₜ = marginal PD in period t (conditional on survival to t)

Multiple Economic Scenarios (IFRS 9 para 5.5.17)
ECL = Σ (Probability_s × ECL_s) across scenarios s
Minimum: Base, Upside, and Downside scenarios weighted by assigned probability

Chapter 07

Advanced Risk Concepts

Beyond day-to-day management, credit risk professionals must understand capital adequacy, stress testing, risk appetite frameworks, and economic capital allocation.

Economic Capital & Risk-Adjusted Return

Economic Capital (EC)
Capital estimated internally (not by regulator) to absorb unexpected losses at a confidence level of typically 99.9%. Equals the difference between loss VaR at 99.9% and expected loss.
EC = VaR(99.9%) − EL
RAROC
Risk-Adjusted Return on Capital. Compares risk-adjusted income to economic capital consumed. Enables apples-to-apples comparison of profitability across business lines and products.
RAROC = (NII − EL − OpEx + FTP) / EC
EVA — Economic Value Added
Profit above the cost of capital. Positive EVA means the business creates shareholder value; negative destroys it.
EVA = (RAROC − WACC) × Economic Capital
FTP — Funds Transfer Pricing
Internal pricing mechanism allocating funding costs and benefits between treasury and business units. Crucial for correct RAROC and product profitability measurement.
Risk Appetite Framework
Board-approved statement defining the level and types of risk the institution is willing to accept in pursuit of its strategy. Cascades into quantitative limits for NPL, Gini minimums, score band caps, and loss tolerances.
ICAAP
Internal Capital Adequacy Assessment Process. Annual board-owned document demonstrating the bank has assessed all material risks and holds adequate capital under base and stressed scenarios.

Stress Testing Framework

1
Scenario Design
Define macroeconomic shocks: GDP contraction, unemployment spike, house price decline, interest rate jump. Regulatory scenarios (PRA ACS, EBA, Fed DFAST) provide standardised baselines. Banks add internal scenarios for idiosyncratic risks.
2
Satellite Model Mapping
PD, LGD, and EAD satellite models translate macroeconomic variables into risk parameter changes. For example: +1% unemployment → +X basis points in PD. Typically estimated using regression on historical cycle data.
3
Loss Projection
Stressed PD/LGD/EAD applied to the current portfolio to project losses over 3–5 years. Roll-rate or transition matrix models project staging movements and provision requirements under each scenario.
4
Capital Impact Assessment
Projected losses deducted from starting CET1 capital. CET1 ratio trajectory assessed across scenarios. Management actions evaluated. Results inform ICAAP and Recovery & Resolution Plan (RRP).

TTC vs. PIT PD — Cyclicality

📅

Through-the-Cycle (TTC) PD

  • Average PD across a full economic cycle
  • Less volatile; does not spike in downturns
  • Required for Basel IRB regulatory capital
  • Prevents procyclical capital amplification
  • May understate risk in benign environments
📈

Point-in-Time (PIT) PD

  • Current conditions-sensitive PD estimate
  • Incorporates forward macroeconomic outlook
  • Required for IFRS 9 ECL (multiple scenarios)
  • More volatile; rises sharply in recessions
  • Better reflects current risk for provisioning

Modern Credit Modelling

Machine learning is transforming credit scoring and loss forecasting. However, model risk governance, explainability, and fairness remain paramount constraints in regulated lending.

Explainability Tools

SHAP Values
SHapley Additive exPlanations — assigns each feature a contribution to the model prediction using game-theoretic Shapley values. Provides both global feature importance and individual-level case explanation. Standard in ML credit model documentation.
LIME
Local Interpretable Model-agnostic Explanations. Fits a simple linear model locally around each prediction. Useful for generating adverse action notice reasons in ML-driven decisioning.
Partial Dependence Plots
Show marginal effect of one or two features on model output, averaged over all other features. Reveals monotonicity violations — e.g. higher income → higher risk — which are conceptually unsound and must be corrected.
Adverse Action Notices
US ECOA / Regulation B requirement to provide declined applicants with the specific top reasons for adverse action. Mandates feature-level explainability in any scoring model, including ML.

Model Risk Management

Governed by PRA SS1/23 (UK) and Federal Reserve SR 11-7 (US). The model lifecycle has four mandatory phases:

D
Development (Model Owner — 1st Line)
Full documentation: purpose, data lineage, methodology, assumptions, limitations, performance metrics. Includes out-of-time (OOT) and out-of-sample (OOS) validation. Champion-challenger framework defined before deployment.
V
Independent Validation (Model Risk — 2nd Line)
Second-line challenge of methodology, data, and results. Replication testing. Conceptual soundness review. Benchmarking against alternative models. Formal written opinion issued with findings and conditions.
A
Approval (Model Risk Committee)
Governance committee reviews validation opinion and approves model for production use with conditions. Material models require CRO or Board Risk Committee sign-off. Model registered in model inventory with tiering.
M
Ongoing Monitoring (Monthly Performance Pack)
Gini decay, PSI, KS, calibration error tracked monthly. Trigger-based escalation for threshold breaches. Annual full review mandatory. Model risk tiering (Tier 1 = highest criticality) determines review frequency and depth.

Fairness & Bias

Regulatory Risk — Protected Characteristics

Credit models must not discriminate on protected characteristics (gender, race, religion, age, disability) under the Equality Act 2010 (UK), Equal Credit Opportunity Act (US), and equivalent legislation. Disparate impact testing must be embedded in model development and ongoing monitoring. Proxy discrimination — using features highly correlated with protected characteristics — is equally prohibited and must be tested.

Disparate Impact
When a model disproportionately declines, prices higher, or assigns lower limits to a protected group, even without explicit use of the protected characteristic. Must be tested at development and in ongoing monitoring.
4/5ths Rule (US)
US EEOC guideline: adverse impact indicated if the selection rate for a protected group is less than 80% of the rate for the group with the highest selection rate. Widely applied in credit fairness testing.
GDPR Article 22 (EU/UK)
Right not to be subject solely to automated decision-making that produces significant effects. Requires the right to human review and meaningful explanation of automated credit decisions.
Fairness Metrics
Demographic parity, equalised odds, individual fairness, calibration within groups. No single metric is universally optimal — choice depends on the regulatory and ethical context of the specific model use.

  • Basel III / CRR2/CRR3 (EU): Capital requirements — risk weights, IRB approaches, Basel IV output floor (72.5% from 2028)
  • IFRS 9 (IASB): Financial instruments — ECL model, three-stage classification, significant increase in credit risk
  • ASC 326 / CECL (US GAAP): Current Expected Credit Loss — US equivalent of IFRS 9 ECL, effective for most banks from 2023
  • EBA GL/2020/06: EBA Guidelines on loan origination and monitoring — origination standards, creditworthiness assessment
  • FCA Consumer Duty (PS22/9): Good outcomes for retail customers — pricing, collections, and vulnerable customer treatment
  • PRA SS1/23: Model risk management expectations for UK banks — lifecycle, governance, documentation
